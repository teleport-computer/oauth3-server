roadmap snapshot

What we're building, and why it's three layers.

A snapshot of the oauth3 + tee-daemon design as it stands today. One idea, drawn out across a set of RFCs: you should own how much of your own credential you hand out, and a TEE you trust should enforce it.

2026-06-25 · design snapshot, not a spec · most of this is written down, not yet built · detail lives in the RFCs

the one idea

Most of the web has no OAuth. To let an app use your account you hand it your whole cookie — and everything that cookie can do. OAuth1 and OAuth2 let a provider hand out their fixed scopes. OAuth3 lets you set the dial, on your own credential, anywhere from a narrow read to the raw thing, with a TEE you control holding the credential and enforcing the setting.

one primitive, two axes

There is one thing in the system: a delegation. Not two kinds ("adapter" that touches the credential vs "app" that consumes output) — those are soft conventions on a continuum. A delegation has two independent axes, and how much friction it takes to approve is a function of both.

Success here isn't "least privilege". It's legibility: high power is fine when it's explicit and honest. "Reads all your watch history, and nothing else" is a good grant. The curator's job is to make sure the words an app shows you are ones it can't exceed.

authorization is three layers

layer 1Listing & curation — which apps may exist. An agentic curator vets an app once, in a language of capability statements, and earns trust through a cheap→premium verification portfolio.
layer 2Per-user grant — a listed app still needs you to approve it onto your own data. connect() → a scoped, revocable token. Listing is not access. (Live today.)
layer 3Runtime step-up — even with a valid token, a risky read can pause and ask you out-of-band. The credit-card fraud-check model: the issuer decides, not the app.

one discipline, three feedback loops

Wherever there's a cheap-but-imperfect default and a better-but-expensive path, we keep the default and spend on the expensive path only where it pays — then collect feedback so we need it less over time. The same shape shows up three times, spending three different resources.

two layers, two repos

The work splits cleanly. oauth3 is the application layer — identity, delegation, the curator's language. tee-daemon (dstack-webhost) is the platform — the TEE, attestation, the credential broker, the curated-list machinery. The app RFCs consume the platform RFCs; they don't re-implement them.

how it feels — across your devices

The extension is the only thing that can put a cookie in. Once a jar is sealed in the instance, every other device reads it through a linked sign-in, holding only a scoped token.

and across operators

Other people run their own pods. The same app, by its code measurement, can run on yours and theirs. A data room holds members' data under a policy that says which code may touch it; admitting a new version, a peer pod, or a visiting ambassador is one governed operation, and any member can opt out of it — a fine-grained "rage quit" of changes they don't accept (generalizing Nerla Jean-Louis's dstack update-opt-out work). An ambassador is just a delegation across operators: the host verifies the visitor's code; the visitor verifies the host's. Data either stays in place and is computed on, or is re-encrypted only to peers a member hasn't opted out of.
