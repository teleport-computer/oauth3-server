roadmap snapshot

interoperability for all

Use any account you have with the apps and agents you choose, on your terms. oauth3 keeps your credentials sealed in a TEE and hands each app a narrow, revocable slice — reaching any site you can log into, through your cookies, or a browser in the enclave for sites with no API.

2026-06-25 · a design snapshot, not a spec · most of it is written down, not built yet · each part links to its RFC

the dial

To let an app use one of your accounts today, you hand it the whole login cookie, and everything that cookie can do. OAuth1 and OAuth2 only let the provider hand out the scopes the provider defined. oauth3 puts a control between you and the app. The credential stays sealed in a TEE, and the app gets a token scoped to whatever you allow: a single read, one category of data, or the full credential if that is what you want. You set the amount, and the TEE enforces it.

what an app does with your data

Before an app is listed, a checker reads its code and writes down what it does, in plain words: reads your full watch history, writes nothing, sends it only to your TinyCloud. The app runs attested, so what it actually does is held to that description and cannot exceed it. How hard the check is depends on how much the app asks for and how far its code can be verified.

The aim is not to keep every app on a short leash. A lot of access is fine when it is stated plainly and you can confirm the statement is true.

reaching your accounts from another device

The extension is the only thing that puts a credential into the pod. Once it is sealed there, any device reads it through a sign-in you have linked — Google, GitHub, or a Matrix homeserver — holding only a token. The credential never copies to the second device. The laptop with the extension is where credentials go in; everything else signs in and reads.

using someone else's data without moving it

Other people run their own pods. The same app, identified by its code, runs on yours and theirs. A data room holds members' data under a policy that records which code is allowed to touch it. Admitting a new version of the app, a peer's pod, or a visiting agent is the same operation, and every member can decline it, per record, before their data is included. This builds on existing work on opting out of TEE app upgrades.

spend effort where it pays, and learn

One design idea runs through three parts of the system. Each has a cheap way to decide and a slow, expensive way to decide. It uses the cheap way by default and pays for the expensive one only where it is worth it. Then it records what it decided and what happened, so the cheap way gets better and the expensive one is needed less. The more the system is used, the cheaper and more accurate it gets — it is built to improve as it runs, not to be tuned by hand.

approval that starts cautious and grows

What an app or agent needs your sign-off for depends on how reversible the action is and who it touches. Something reversible that affects only its own work, it does. Something reversible that touches your other data, it proposes and waits. Something irreversible that affects other people, it can only draft, and you decide. It starts cautious and earns a wider berth as it proves itself, one kind of action at a time. The aim is not to ask about everything, or to act on everything, but to keep that balance right and keep adjusting it.

the pieces

Underneath, the work splits across two repositories. oauth3-server is the application layer and deploys as a project onto the platform. tee-daemon (dstack-webhost) is the platform: the TEE, attestation, the credential broker, the curated-list machinery. The application RFCs build on the platform ones.
